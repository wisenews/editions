The klesha

Wonder, as a klesha, manifests as a captivating pull, a subtle mental grasping at the new, profound, or unexpected. It often feels like an expansive opening in the chest, a quickening of attention, accompanied by a cascade of thoughts and interpretations. While initially delightful, this mental activity, if unchecked, can lead to subtle fixation, obscuring the open, unconditioned nature of experience.

The wisdom it reveals

Dharmadhatu Wisdom reveals the boundless, unconditioned expanse of reality, where all phenomena arise and dissolve without inherent solidity. It's the knowing that directly perceives the profound interconnectedness and sunyata (emptiness) of all experience, cutting through the conceptual overlays that often accompany our sense of wonder. This wisdom allows us to rest in the open, luminous ground of being, beyond fixation or grasping.

How to work with it
Abandoning Apply the antidote — reduce and release
To work with wonder through abandoning: When you notice the captivating pull of wonder, that subtle mental grasping or proliferation around a new or profound experience, pause. Bring your attention gently to the sensation of your breath in your lower abdomen, feeling the rise and fall. Allow this physical anchor to ground you. Recognise the mental energy of wonder – the thoughts, the 'wow,' the desire to analyse or hold onto the experience – as simply another stream of mind. Instead of following it, softly redirect your attention back to the breath, or to the simple sensation of your feet on the floor. For a few breaths, perhaps five to ten, consciously release the mental narratives surrounding wonder. Let the experience itself be just as it is, without embellishment or conceptual overlay. Conclude by offering this moment of release for the benefit of all beings, that they too may find freedom from subtle fixation.
Transforming Use the energy as fuel — redirect toward wisdom
To work with wonder through transformation: When wonder arises, notice its expansive, open quality in your mind and body – perhaps a lightness in the chest, a heightened sense of perception. Instead of letting this energy dissipate into mental stories or subtle grasping, consciously gather it. Recognise that this very capacity for awe and appreciation is a precious quality of awareness. Now, connect this open, vibrant energy of wonder with bodhichitta. Imagine the wonder you feel for a scientific breakthrough or an act of kindness, expanding beyond your personal experience. Let that expansive feeling radiate outwards, like a warm, luminous current, encompassing all beings. Wish that all may experience such moments of profound connection and clarity. Allow the energy of wonder to fuel your aspiration for their awakening, transforming a personal appreciation into boundless compassion. Hold this intention for a few moments, feeling the energy shift from self-focused awe to altruistic aspiration.
Transcending Look directly — allow self-liberation
To work with wonder through transcendence: When wonder arises, instead of engaging with its content or trying to manage its energy, turn your awareness directly towards the experience of wonder itself. Ask: 'What is this wonder? Where does it reside? Who is wondering?' Don't search for a conceptual answer, but rather rest in open, present awareness and gently investigate the felt sense. Look for a solid, inherent 'thing' called wonder. Can you pinpoint its location in your body or mind? Does it have a fixed shape, color, or duration? As you look directly, you'll find that wonder, like all mental phenomena, is vivid in its appearing but utterly ungraspable in its essence. It arises without coming from anywhere, dwells without remaining, and dissolves without going anywhere. Rest in this recognition of its vibrant emptiness, its tsal (display of awareness) that is simultaneously rang grol (self-liberated). Allow the experience of wonder to simply be, without grasping or rejecting, naturally dissolving back into the vastness of rigpa, its true nature.

I was bathed with acid meant for my brother – Anambra graduate
What has happened
Obanye Francess, a Biochemistry graduate from Anambra, Nigeria, became an acid attack survivor at age nine when she was mistakenly targeted instead of her brother. The attack, stemming from a family conflict, left her with severe burns. Despite immense physical pain, emotional trauma, and societal discrimination, Francess completed her education. She now operates an online dropshipping business, diversifying her income to overcome employment challenges faced by burn survivors. She also recently experienced the loss of both her parents.
Punch Newspapers ↗
Wisdom teaching
✦
The story of Obanye Francess, who endured an acid attack meant for another, is heartbreaking. Her journey through immense physical pain, emotional trauma, and societal discrimination, yet still completing her education, reveals an indomitable spirit. It's easy to feel anger or despair when faced with such senseless violence, such ma rigpa (ignorance). But within this very suffering, if we look closely, we find the raw, untamed energy of compassion. This isn't a soft, gentle feeling that avoids pain. It's a fierce, courageous willingness to face the reality of suffering, both hers and our own, and to connect with the profound shared vulnerability of being human. Francess's resilience itself is a teaching, a testament to the mind's capacity to find strength amidst the most horrific circumstances. How do we meet such suffering without being overwhelmed?

It’s the tsal (display, energy) of rigpa manifesting as courage.
