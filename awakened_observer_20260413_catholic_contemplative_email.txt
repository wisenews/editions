  [GRIEF & WITNESSING]
  Nigerian Air Force Kills Over 100 Civilians at Weekly Market

  A Nigerian Air Force jet strike aimed at Boko Haram militants
  hit the Jilli weekly market in Yobe State on Saturday, killing
  at least 100 civilians and injuring scores more, including
  children. The Yobe State Government confirmed the military was
  targeting a jihadist stronghold near the market when people who
  had come to trade were caught in the blast. Amnesty
  International, which verified the toll from survivors and
  medical sources, has called for an independent investigation.
  Some local accounts put the death toll as high as 200.

  ✦

  They came to sell bread, not to wage war. They came carrying
  yams, bolts of cloth, the small transactions that hold a
  community together. One hundred of them are dead now — caught
  not in a battle but in a market. Jesus spent most of his
  ministry in places like this: busy with commerce, alive with the
  daily weight of earning and feeding and living.

  There is a line in Matthew that returns here: 'Whatever you did
  for the least of these, you did for me.' Not symbolically —
  literally. The people of the Jilli market were the least of
  these. Their deaths are not distant news. They are a direct
  address to anyone who claims to follow the Gospel.

  What do we do with this? Not guilt, which collapses into
  paralysis. But the metanoia the Gospel asks of us: a genuine
  turning. A decision to see these deaths, to name them, and to
  let them cost us something — even in small ways — as those who
  believe every human life is sacred beyond measure.
  — In the spirit of the Gospel of Matthew's teaching on the dignity of the least of these

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  SOCRATES: Teacher, you speak of the sacred dignity of these
  lives. But the commanders who ordered the strike also believe
  they are protecting sacred lives — their own people. How do we
  reason between these competing claims?
  JESUS: In the kingdom I am describing, there is no 'our
  people' that excludes 'their people.' The mother who came to
  that market with a basket of tomatoes — she is my people. The
  soldier who released the weapon, believing he was keeping his
  own children safe — he too is my people. The question is not
  whose side God is on. The question is whether we are willing
  to build a world where no decision requires choosing who
  matters.
  SOCRATES: Is that achievable?
  JESUS: It is the only project worth attempting. Everything
  else is just politics with better weapons.
  — In the spirit of the Gospel's teaching on universal agape,
  and Socrates' examination of justice and the examined life

  PRACTICE  [Grief & Loss]

  Find a quiet moment and open your hands in your lap — the
  gesture of receiving. Breathe slowly. Bring to mind the people
  of the Jilli market: the sellers, the buyers, the children at
  the edge of the stalls. Do not look away. Let their faces —
  imagined, unknown — be real to you.

  Ask yourself one question, slowly: What would it mean to believe
  that each of these lives is as precious as my own? Sit with that
  for three minutes. Let it be a prayer that doesn't require a
  quick answer.

  KINGDOM OF GOD VIEW VIEW

  The kingdom of God is not a future state. Jesus announced it as
  present, available, breaking in — 'the kingdom of God is at
  hand.' But at hand to whom, and where?

  In Matthew 25, Jesus locates the kingdom precisely: among the
  hungry, the stranger, the prisoner, the sick. Not in palaces or
  parliaments. In the market at Jilli, Yobe State, where people
  came to buy and sell the small necessities of life, the kingdom
  was present — in the transactions of ordinary human dignity, in
  the choosing and counting of coins, in the companionship of the
  weekly gathering.

  The bomb fell on the kingdom. Not on an abstraction. On the
  specific place where Jesus said he would be found.

  This is not theodicy — an attempt to explain why God permits
  such things. It is a statement about where our attention must
  go. If the kingdom is among the poor and the vulnerable, then
  the church's vocation is not first to explain suffering but to
  be present to it, to name it, and to work — with whatever tools
  are available — toward a world that does not make such choices.
  — Kingdom of God view: the kingdom present in the marketplace, among the ordinary and the poor

  [GRIEF & WITNESSING]
  Russia and Ukraine Accuse Each Other of 2,000+ Easter Ceasefire
  Violations

  A 32-hour ceasefire declared by President Putin for Orthodox
  Easter broke down within hours, with Ukraine's military
  reporting 2,299 violations — including 747 attack drone strikes
  and 1,045 FPV drone strikes — by Sunday morning. Russia's
  Defense Ministry also claimed 1,971 violations by Ukrainian
  forces. Both countries continued shelling and drone campaigns
  throughout the Easter period, and civilians were reported killed
  on the Russian side of the border. A similar truce last Easter
  also collapsed into mutual accusations.

  ✦

  Easter is the feast of impossibility made real. And yet within
  the first hour of the truce, 747 drone strikes. Russia and
  Ukraine together logged nearly four thousand ceasefire
  violations before Sunday Mass had ended. What does the
  Resurrection mean in a world where even its feast cannot hold a
  ceasefire?

  It means precisely what it always meant: not that suffering
  ends, but that it does not have the final word. The Church has
  no army, no drone fleet. But she has a voice, and that voice
  must say clearly: this is a desecration. The deliberate
  continuation of violence on the holiest day of the Christian
  year, whatever uniform one wears, is a sin against humanity and
  against hope itself.

  And yet the deeper question remains for each of us: what
  ceasefire can I hold within my own life, my own heart? John
  XXIII wrote that peace is not merely the absence of war. It is
  an order built by love. That order must begin now — in us — in
  the only moment we have.
  — In the spirit of Catholic social teaching on peace, conscience, and the dignity of every human person

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  RUMI: The lovers of God do not divide the world into holy days
  and ordinary days. Every breath is a feast. If the feast can
  be bombed, it was never held in the right place to begin with.
  POPE LEO: You are saying the ceasefire must be interior before
  it can be diplomatic?
  RUMI: I am saying that a peace treaty signed only on paper has
  no roots. The peace that holds in a bombardment is the peace
  that has been grown in the heart over thirty years of
  practice.
  POPE LEO: And yet we must also sign the treaties. We must
  negotiate, build institutions, establish law. The interior
  life does not excuse us from the work of structures.
  RUMI: No. But institutions that are not fed by the interior
  life become shells. Look at the Security Council. Shells.
  POPE LEO: Then we need both — the mystic and the diplomat.
  Perhaps in the same person.
  RUMI: Perhaps in this moment, in the person reading these
  words.
  — In the spirit of Rumi's teaching on the peace that outlasts
  all treaties, and Catholic social teaching on the structures
  of a just peace

  PRACTICE  [Helplessness & Overwhelm]

  In the tradition of lectio divina, take this line from the
  Easter liturgy slowly: 'Do not be afraid. He is not here. He has
  risen.' Read it once. Then read it again and let it touch the
  specific fear or grief you are carrying today.

  On a third reading, ask: What in me needs to rise? What am I
  ready to let die so that something truer can live? Sit in
  silence for five minutes. This is resurrection as practice, not
  merely as belief.

  KENOSIS VIEW VIEW

  Kenosis is Paul's word in Philippians 2: Christ 'emptied
  himself, taking the form of a servant.' It is the theological
  name for the self-emptying that makes genuine encounter with the
  other possible.

  Every failed ceasefire is a failure of kenosis. Each side clings
  to its grievances, its strategic advantage, its narrative of
  victimhood and justified response. The 2,299 violations are not
  crimes of aggression only — they are acts of self-preservation,
  of refusing to be the one who gives first.

  Kenosis does not mean unilateral surrender. Thomas Merton was
  careful here: genuine self-emptying is not self-destruction. It
  is the release of the defended self, the one that cannot afford
  to be wrong, that cannot afford to give without a guarantee of
  reciprocation.

  What would a kenotic foreign policy look like? We do not know —
  it has never been tried at scale. But every peace that has ever
  held has contained its element: the moment when one party chose,
  against strategic logic, to extend the hand without knowing if
  it would be taken. Easter is the feast of that gesture. And so
  the ceasefire was declared. And so it broke. And so Easter comes
  again next year.
  — Kenosis view: self-emptying as the theological ground of peace — the gesture offered without guarantee of return

  [RENEWAL & TRANSFORMATION]
  Hungary's Péter Magyar Defeats Viktor Orbán, Ending 16 Years of
  Authoritarian Rule

  Hungary's parliamentary election on April 12 produced a historic
  result: opposition leader Péter Magyar's Tisza party won 53.6%
  of the vote, securing 138 of 199 parliamentary seats — enough
  for a supermajority — while Viktor Orbán's Fidesz took just 55
  seats with 37.8%. Turnout reached 77%, the highest since
  Hungary's post-communist era. Orbán, in power for 16 consecutive
  years and long seen as a symbol of European democratic
  backsliding, conceded defeat on election night. Magyar is
  expected to reverse many of Orbán's constitutional changes and
  restore Hungary's ties to the European Union.

  ✦

  Merton spent years in a monastery in Kentucky, watching the
  world from what he called 'the margin of history.' But he was
  never indifferent to it. When he heard about Budapest in 1956,
  he wept. He believed contemplative life was not a withdrawal
  from history but a different way of being fully inside it.

  In Hungary, 77 percent of the people came out and chose
  differently. After sixteen years, something in the national body
  remembered its own dignity. Merton would recognize this. He
  called it the 'true self' — not the self constructed by power
  and fear and years of managed reality, but the self that knows
  it is free because it is held in something greater than any
  government.

  Elections are not, in themselves, awakening. But something in
  this one moves in the direction of the true self: the refusal to
  keep pretending that what is happening is acceptable. That
  refusal — en masse, on a ballot — is not kenosis. But it rhymes
  with it. It is the beginning of an unlearning.
  — In the spirit of Thomas Merton's teaching on the true self and the contemplative engagement with history

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Brother Thomas, in your writings I find you
  speak of the 'true self' as something to be discovered, while
  in our teaching we say there is no fixed self to find. How do
  you hold that?
  THOMAS MERTON: I'm not sure I reconcile them — I suspect they
  are pointing at the same territory from different angles. What
  I call the 'true self' is not a solid thing. It is more like
  what remains when all the false constructions fall away.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Yes. The awareness that remains when
  everything added to it dissolves.
  THOMAS MERTON: And what happened in Hungary is that sixteen
  years of constructed fear began to fall away, and people found
  they were still there beneath it. Still capable of choosing.
  Still free.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: The awakening does not create freedom. It
  reveals the freedom that was always present.
  THOMAS MERTON: That is a better way to say it than anything I
  have written.
  — In the spirit of Thomas Merton's lifelong dialogue with
  Buddhism, and Mingyur Rinpoche's teachings on the nature of
  awareness

  PRACTICE  [Hope & Renewal]

  Merton wrote: 'The beginning of contemplation is the beginning
  of freedom.' Today, recall a moment in your own life when you
  chose differently — when you refused, quietly, to go along with
  what you knew to be false. Not a dramatic act. A small one.

  Take two minutes and sit with that memory. Honor it. Let it
  remind you who you are beneath what you perform for the world.
  That person — the one who refused — is closer to your true self
  than anything else you have done today.

  KINGDOM OF GOD VIEW VIEW

  The kingdom of God, in Jesus's teaching, is not imposed from
  above. It grows — like a mustard seed, like leaven in dough —
  from within the ordinary substance of human life. It is not a
  government program. But sometimes it moves through governments.

  When 77 percent of a people, after sixteen years, choose
  differently — not out of optimism but out of a deep refusal to
  continue consenting to what they know is wrong — something in
  that refusal is consonant with the kingdom. Not identical to it.
  But consonant.

  The language of the Gospel for this is metanoia: the turning of
  the whole person, the reversal of direction. In Hungary,
  something like collective metanoia took place. Not religious
  metanoia — political. But the structure is the same: the
  recognition that what has been accepted as inevitable is not, in
  fact, inevitable. That another way is possible. That the door
  that appeared to be sealed is, actually, open.

  'Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness' —
  not for comfort, not for stability, but for what is right. In
  Hungary, a great many people were hungry and thirsty. They ate
  and drank on April 12th.
  — Kingdom of God view: collective metanoia as a political form of the Gospel's deepest invitation

  [POWER & COLLECTIVE KARMA]
  Trump Orders U.S. Naval Blockade of Strait of Hormuz After Iran
  Talks Collapse

  President Trump ordered the U.S. Navy to begin blockading the
  Strait of Hormuz effective Monday after U.S.-Iran peace talks in
  Islamabad collapsed on Sunday. Roughly 20% of the world's oil
  and natural gas transits the strait, and markets responded with
  Brent crude prices jumping 10–13% in early trading. U.S. Central
  Command clarified the blockade targets ships paying tolls to
  Iran, not all vessels using the waterway. The United Kingdom
  declined to participate, stating the strait is 'urgently needed
  to support the global economy,' while Russia and China vetoed a
  UN Security Council resolution on the crisis.

  ✦

  Francis once walked to Egypt in the middle of the Crusades to
  speak with Sultan Malik al-Kamil. He brought no weapons, only
  his willingness to be in the presence of the other. The Sultan
  received him with courtesy. They talked for days. Nothing was
  resolved politically, but something human happened between them
  that armies had not managed in years of fighting.

  Now the Strait of Hormuz is closed, and a 10 percent jump in oil
  prices means that someone, somewhere, will go without — will
  heat their home less, cut the food budget, face a choice they
  shouldn't have to make. Creation is not abstract. The poor are
  not abstract. They feel the tremors of decisions made in rooms
  they will never enter.

  Francis believed that voluntary simplicity — the love of poverty
  freely chosen — was not self-punishment but freedom. A freedom
  that makes the blockade of a strait matter less to you, because
  you have already loosened your grip on what you think you need.
  This is not a political strategy. But it is the contemplative
  ground from which better politics can grow.
  — In the spirit of Saint Francis of Assisi's teaching on holy poverty, Sister Creation, and peace as a personal vocation

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  DALAI LAMA: Brother Francis, I have always admired that you
  went to the Sultan during the Crusades. Were you not afraid?
  ST. FRANCIS: Terrified. And the terror was less important than
  the going. I believed — I still believe — that if you walk
  toward the other without weapons, something must happen that
  cannot happen any other way.
  DALAI LAMA: The hand extended in openness cannot hold a sword.
  This is why, even with China, I have insisted on dialogue. Not
  because I believe it will immediately succeed. But refusing
  dialogue closes the only door.
  ST. FRANCIS: And in the Strait of Hormuz today — what door
  remains open?
  DALAI LAMA: The door that is always open: the willingness to
  accept a little suffering for the sake of the other's
  suffering. To say — I will give something up so that you might
  have something. In your tradition, this is agape. In mine, it
  is compassion. Different words for the same gesture.
  ST. FRANCIS: Then perhaps what is needed is not a navy but a
  different kind of pilgrim.
  — In the spirit of Saint Francis's teaching on peace as a
  personal vocation, and the Dalai Lama's teaching on compassion
  as political wisdom

  PRACTICE  [Anxiety & Uncertainty]

  Francis prayed: 'Lord, make me an instrument of your peace.' Not
  an observer of peace, or a mourner of its absence — an
  instrument.

  Today, identify one specific thing you actually control — in
  your household, your spending, your consumption — that you could
  loosen your grip on. Not out of guilt. Out of freedom. Even a
  small act of voluntary simplicity, chosen consciously, is a form
  of prayer. It is also, quietly, a refusal of the logic that
  closed the strait.

  KENOSIS VIEW VIEW

  The blockade of the Strait of Hormuz is, at its root, an act of
  the defended self writ large: the self that cannot tolerate
  uncertainty, that responds to failed negotiation with the
  assertion of control, that mistakes power for security.

  Kenosis moves in the opposite direction. It releases the claim
  to control what cannot ultimately be controlled. It discovers —
  not through naivety but through genuine inner transformation —
  that security is not a function of domination.

  This sounds impractical in geopolitics. And yet: the most
  durable peace agreements in history have contained this element.
  Camp David. The Good Friday Agreement. Moments when leaders
  chose, against every strategic calculation, to let go of
  something they believed essential to their security — and
  discovered that the other side, also terrified, also defended,
  could respond in kind.

  Anxiety is the klesha of the defended self. It arises from the
  belief that if we loosen our grip, we will lose everything.
  Kenosis says: loosen the grip. Not because the world is safe,
  but because the defended self never finds the security it seeks.
  The Strait will still be there. But what you are will have
  changed.
  — Kenosis view: releasing the defended self as the ground of a security that domination can never provide
