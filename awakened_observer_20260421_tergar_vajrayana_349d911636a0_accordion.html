The klesha

Aversion manifests as a visceral tightening, a recoiling from what is unwanted, often accompanied by a sharp, pushing-away energy in the body and mind. It's the urgent "no" to an experience, a person, or a situation, frequently felt as a knot in the stomach, a clenching jaw, or a burning sensation, compelling us to create distance or eliminate the perceived threat.

The wisdom it reveals

Mirror-like Wisdom reveals the pristine, unbiased clarity that perceives all phenomena exactly as they are, without distortion or judgment. It's the inherent capacity to reflect everything impartially, much like a flawless mirror simply shows what is presented, illuminating the precise nature of what aversion attempts to push away. This wisdom offers the stable, unshakeable ground from which to see the raw energy of aversion without being caught in its reactive spin.

How to work with it
Abandoning Apply the antidote — reduce and release
To work with aversion through abandoning: When aversion arises—perhaps a surge of irritation reading about the "Zelensky Says Failure of US Envoys to Visit Kyiv Is Disrespectful" story, or a general sense of being fed up—pause. Instead of immediately reacting or pushing away, anchor your attention softly on your breath, feeling it in your belly. Notice the specific sensations of aversion in your body: is it tightness, heat, a clenching? Acknowledge it without judgment, simply as a pattern of energy. Then, consciously soften around these sensations. Breathe in, allowing the feeling to be present; breathe out, releasing any tension you might be holding in resistance to it. You might silently repeat a phrase like, "May I be free from suffering," or "May I be at ease." Continue this gentle breathing and softening, directing a quiet, spacious awareness toward the aversion. Do this for several minutes, or until you feel a subtle shift, a slight opening or release. Conclude by dedicating this moment of gentle presence to the benefit of all beings caught in the grip of aversion.
Transforming Use the energy as fuel — redirect toward wisdom
To work with aversion through transformation: When aversion grips you, perhaps a strong dislike for a political figure or a situation like the "Trump Extends US-Iran Ceasefire" news, feel the raw energy of that pushing-away. Instead of resisting it, try to identify its core impulse: often, it's a profound wish for things to be different, for suffering to cease. Acknowledge this deep longing. Now, turn that intense energy, that "I don't want this," into a powerful aspiration for bodhichitta. Using tonglen, breathe in the discomfort, the sharp edge of your own aversion, and the suffering it represents in the world. As you breathe in, imagine taking in the aversion of others, their frustration, their anger, their pain. Hold it with an open heart. As you breathe out, send out spaciousness, peace, understanding, and the wish for freedom from suffering to yourself and all beings. Feel the energy of aversion transmute into compassionate engagement, fueling your resolve to work for a world where such suffering is less prevalent, not by pushing away, but by embracing with wisdom and kindness.
Transcending Look directly — allow self-liberation
To work with aversion through transcendence: When aversion arises, perhaps a strong reaction to the "Language Documentation Needs Community Rights" interview, notice its presence. Instead of getting caught in its narrative or trying to change it, simply look directly at the aversion itself. Where is it? Does it have a solid location in your body or mind? What is its colour, shape, texture? Is it truly a "thing" you can grasp? As you investigate, hold your attention with an open, non-conceptual awareness, resting in rigpa. You're not trying to make it go away; you're simply looking. What you'll likely discover is that aversion, when directly scrutinised, lacks inherent existence. It's a dynamic play of energy, an ephemeral movement of mind (tsal) without a fixed core. As you rest in this direct seeing, the aversion naturally begins to relax its grip, dissolving back into the vast, open space of awareness (kunzhi). It's not abandoned or transformed, but recognised as sunyata, self-liberating (rang grol) in the very act of being seen without conceptual overlay. This direct recognition is the essence of Mahamudra.

Hear the word Zelensky chose: disrespectful. Not unjust, not cruel — unseen. There is a particular grief that arises not from the blow itself but from the turning away of the one who could have helped, and who chose instead to sit at another table. This grief has a long lineage. The Bodhicaryavatara teaches that when we truly take up the burden of others, we cease to ask who is equivalent to whom — we simply feel where the pain is and move toward it. Second, to ask of ourselves: whom, in our own lives, have we declined to visit? Whose grief have we treated as one voice among many, when it was asking only to be met?

The Surprising Origin of 4 Features That Superglue Kids — and Adults — to Screens
What has happened
New reporting reveals that four design features now ubiquitous in social media and gaming — variable reward schedules, social validation loops, infinite scroll, and deliberate removal of stopping cues — were intentionally engineered to induce a trancelike state maximizing time on platform. Many of these mechanisms were adapted from slot machine psychology and behavioral conditioning research. Children's developing brains are particularly susceptible to the dopaminergic conditioning these features exploit. The findings reframe screen overuse not as personal weakness but as exposure to a designed environment built specifically to defeat willpower.

Plain speech for a plain matter: the pull you feel toward the little bright rectangle is not your weakness. It was built. Slot-machine variables, social pellets, scrolls without end, the quiet removal of any moment that says stop — these were designed, by paid engineers, to hold you in a trance. Children especially. Knowing this is not a cause for shame, but it is a cause for waking up. The dug lnga teach that craving is not the problem; the problem is craving that does not know it is craving. There is a difference between the archivist who kneels before the elder and the scraper that strips her voice for a dataset. The first is preservation. The second is extraction dressed as care. Taukeer Alam, writing in Van Gujjari, is pointing at something the dharma has always known: that a living tradition cannot be separated from the bodies and communities that carry it, any more than a breath can be separated from the lungs that give it.
