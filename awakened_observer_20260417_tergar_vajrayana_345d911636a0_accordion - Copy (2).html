Cross-tradition dialogue
🏛️ socrates  ·  Greek philosophy — Socratic method

SOCRATES: Mingyur, this discovery of ancient sacred water complexes raises a question: What makes something sacred? Is it the thing itself, the water, imbued with intrinsic divine essence? Or is it something we project onto it, a meaning we ascribe from our own minds?

MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Ah, Socrates, a profound inquiry! From the Buddhist view, sacredness is not an inherent quality of an external object. The water is simply water. Yet, through our minds and projections, we imbue elements with sacredness. Like beauty in a painting, it arises from the interaction between art and viewer's mind. It's a quality of our own awareness.

SOCRATES: If sacredness arises from our minds, then is it not a mere illusion? If not truly 'in' the water, is our reverence for these places and practices not ultimately groundless, a form of self-deception? Would it not be more truthful to acknowledge water as merely hydrogen and oxygen, nothing more?

MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Not an illusion, Socrates! While sacredness isn't inherently *in* the water, it isn't 'nothing.' It's a display of our mind's luminous, boundless nature. This shows our minds are active participants in creating reality. Recognizing sacredness arises from mind reveals the profound potential of our own awareness, a key to understanding kunzhi.

Practice prescription
Confusion & Complexity Practice Abandoning
For six minutes, sit comfortably with a glass of water in front of you. First, allow any thoughts or concepts about 'sacred water,' 'cleanliness,' 'thirst,' or 'ancient rituals' to arise and pass without judgment. Simply observe how your mind projects meanings onto the water. Then, gently abandon these conceptual overlays. Rest your attention on the raw sensory experience of the water itself: its color, its movement, its temperature (if you touch it). Allow the mind to simply perceive, without adding any story or label. Experience the water as it is, allowing sacredness to arise from open, non-conceptual awareness. May the clarity emerging from releasing conceptual confusion benefit all beings.
Wisdom aspect: Dharmadhātu Wisdom
💛 Bodhichitta View  ·  Mahayana Buddhist
The discovery of ancient sacred water complexes highlights humanity's enduring connection to the elemental forces of life and our aspiration for deeper meaning. From the bodhichitta perspective, this reminds us that water, like all resources, is precious and essential for the well-being of all beings. Our confusion about its 'sacredness' can be abandoned when we simply recognize its vital role and cultivate the intention to protect and share it equitably, ensuring that all sentient beings have access to what sustains life. This moves us from conceptual attachment to active, compassionate engagement with the practical realities of existence.
— Cultivating universal responsibility for life's essentials and abandoning conceptual confusion
