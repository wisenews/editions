Astronomers Confirm Second-Nearest Habitable Zone Planet at Just 10.7 Light-Years Away
What has happened
A study published in Astronomy & Astrophysics has confirmed GJ 887 d, a super-Earth in the habitable zone of the nearby red dwarf Gliese 887, 10.7 light-years away — making it the second-closest potentially habitable exoplanet known to science. The planet's mass is 6.1 times that of Earth; it orbits every 50.8 days at 0.212 AU from its star. GJ 887 itself is unusually quiet among red dwarfs, with minimal flare activity, raising the prospect that an atmosphere could survive at the planet's surface. No radius measurement or atmospheric data yet exists, so whether it is rocky or a mini-Neptune remains unknown.
phys.org / Astronomy & Astrophysics ↗
✦

Astronomers point their instruments into the dark and find a second address in our cosmic neighborhood — a super-Earth orbiting a quiet star, just close enough to make us lean forward. What strikes me is not the planet itself but the quality of attention that found it. Wonder is like that: a gap in our ordinary busyness where rigpa shines through without effort.

We look for habitable worlds, which really means we look for conditions that can support awareness. But awareness is already here, looking. The search itself is the tsal of mind reaching outward, and what it finds is always a reflection of its own luminous nature.

GJ 887 d may hold an atmosphere. It may not. But the real question the universe keeps posing is not "Is anyone out there?" but "What is this knowing that asks the question?" Ten light-years is close in cosmic terms. Closer still is the distance between you and the awareness reading these words — which is no distance at all.

High Seas Treaty Enters into Force, Placing Half the Planet's Ocean Under Legal Protection
What has happened
The High Seas Treaty (BBNJ Agreement) entered into force on January 17, 2026, after achieving 60 ratifications. It is the first legally binding global framework for protecting biodiversity beyond national jurisdiction — covering approximately half the planet's surface and 95% of ocean volume. The treaty creates mechanisms for designating marine protected areas in international waters, environmental impact assessments, and equitable sharing of marine genetic resource benefits. The treaty opens a path to protecting 30% of the world's oceans by 2030, as part of the global '30 by 30' biodiversity target.
UN News / High Seas Alliance ↗
✦

Sixty nations signed a treaty to protect what belongs to no single nation — the open ocean, the deep water, the creatures that move through international darkness without passports. This is pratityasamutpada made into law. When we understand that my well-being depends on the well-being of the ocean, and the ocean's health depends on restraint I have never practiced before, then protection is not sacrifice. It is intelligence.

Hope is a tricky klesha. It can become passive — waiting for someone else to act. But here, hope has been transformed into structure: legal mechanisms, environmental assessments, shared responsibility. This is what I have always tried to say about compassion. It is not a feeling. It is a policy. It is a treaty. It is sixty governments saying: we will not take everything we can.

The ocean does not know it has been protected. But the beings within it — and we are among them, connected by every breath — may now have a wider margin for survival.

Mediterranean Migrant Deaths Near 1,000 in 2026 as Deadliest Crossing Season Begins
What has happened
At least 990 migrants have died crossing the Mediterranean Sea in 2026, according to the IOM's Missing Migrants Project, with about 765 deaths in the Central Mediterranean alone — more than doubling last year's toll for the same period. In early April, over 180 people died or disappeared in just 10 days. On April 1, 19 migrants were found dead on a vessel off Lampedusa; on April 5, more than 80 went missing when a boat capsized after leaving Tajoura, Libya. Despite a drop in total arrivals, the death rate per crossing is rising sharply as people attempt longer and more dangerous routes.
Al Jazeera / IOM ↗
✦

Nearly one thousand people have died in the Mediterranean this year. Each one stepped onto a boat because the life behind them had become unbearable and the sea, for all its danger, offered more hope than staying. When we read this number — 990 — the mind wants to move quickly past it. The number is too large to feel. This is ma rigpa operating as self-protection: awareness dimming itself because the light of what is real is too bright to bear.

But the practice is not to bear it. The practice is to let one life in. One person on that boat off Lampedusa. One parent holding a child against the waves. If we can stay present with that single image for even ten seconds without turning away, grief does its true work. It breaks the shell of the separate self. It becomes the raw material of bodhichitta.

We cannot save the drowned. But we can refuse the comfort of forgetting them.

Monarch Butterfly Population Surges 64 Percent in Mexico, Largest Recovery Since 2018
What has happened
Monarch butterflies occupied 2.93 hectares of overwintering forest in central Mexico — a 64% increase from 1.79 hectares the previous winter and the highest figure since 2018, according to WWF and Mexico's environmental authorities. The recovery reflects coordinated citizen science monitoring, community-led habitat restoration, and enforcement of illegal logging protections in the Monarch Butterfly Biosphere Reserve. However, scientists note the monarch remains at risk: 7.24 acres of occupied habitat is less than half the 15-acre minimum for the population to be considered safe from migratory collapse.
WWF / Mexico News Daily ↗
✦

Here is something that should stop you mid-sip: no single monarch butterfly has ever completed the full migration. The ones arriving in Mexico this winter are three or four generations removed from the ones that left. The route is not learned. It is not remembered. It moves through bodies that have never been there before, guided by something deeper than individual memory.

In mahamudra, we would say this is kunzhi expressing itself — the ground of being manifesting as a precise, embodied intelligence that does not require a "self" to operate. The butterflies do not know where they are going. And yet they arrive. Every year. Millions of wings enacting a pattern that no single wing has ever seen whole.

The 64 percent recovery is cause for celebration, yes. But the deeper teaching is in the fragility. Even at 2.93 hectares, the population is less than half what scientists consider safe. Wonder and vulnerability travel together. The most astonishing things in this world are also the most easily destroyed. Hold both.

Tibetan Community in London Gathers for Compassion-Inspired Clean-Up at the Tibetan Peace Garden
What has happened
The UK Ghoton Subcommittee organised a compassion-inspired clean-up at the Tibetan Peace Garden, Lambeth, London on April 11, 2026, as part of its 'Year of Compassion' initiative. Over 30 community members gathered to sweep pathways, wash the mandala structure, collect litter, and restore the garden's grounds. The Tibetan Peace Garden was inaugurated by the Dalai Lama in May 1999 and sits adjacent to the Imperial War Museum. The event concluded with a potluck of traditional Tibetan food and tea, and participants joined in a circle dance. The gathering was quiet, deliberate, and rooted in the Tibetan tradition of compassionate service.
Central Tibetan Administration ↗
✦

I remember the day we opened the Tibetan Peace Garden in 1999. It sits beside the Imperial War Museum — this was intentional. Peace does not exist in isolation from the memory of violence. It exists precisely there, in the shadow of what humans have done to each other, as a quiet insistence that we are capable of something else.

Now, twenty-seven years later, thirty Tibetans gather with brooms and buckets. They sweep the paths. They wash the mandala. And then — this is my favorite part — they eat together and dance. This is bodhichitta in its most ordinary and most powerful form. Not a grand gesture. Not a political statement. Just: the garden exists, it needs tending, and we are here.

Compassion in the Year of Compassion looks like this. It looks like soap and water on stone. It looks like people who have lost a homeland caring for a small patch of ground in someone else's country. The mandala they washed is a symbol of the universe. But it is also, today, simply clean.

Compassion & Care Practice Transforming
This is a transforming practice — turning the energy of compassion into embodied action. Choose one small, shared space in your life — a common area, a garden, a street corner, a kitchen that others use. Spend fifteen minutes cleaning it with full attention. As you work, practice tonglen with each gesture: as you pick up litter, breathe in the carelessness that left it there and breathe out the wish that all beings find environments of beauty and care.
