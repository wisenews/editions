The klesha

Grief manifests as a profound sense of loss, often experienced as a heavy ache or hollow space in the chest, a pervasive sadness that colors perception, and a deep longing for what was. It’s the raw, stripped-bare feeling when a significant connection—be it a loved one, a way of life, or even a perceived future—is severed, leaving an emotional landscape altered and often desolate. This can be seen in the struggle of Bolivia's cacao farmers or the quiet revelations of a month offline.

The wisdom it reveals

Discriminating Wisdom, in the face of grief, offers a clear-eyed understanding of what is distinct and unique in our experience, revealing the precise nature of our sorrow without collapsing into a generalized despondency. It helps us discern the specific qualities of what was cherished and what remains, allowing for a nuanced appreciation of impermanence rather than a sweeping rejection. This wisdom clarifies the boundaries of our sadness, preventing it from overwhelming the whole of our being.

How to work with it
Abandoning Apply the antidote — reduce and release
To work with grief through abandoning: Find a comfortable posture and bring your attention to the gentle rise and fall of your breath in your belly. Acknowledge the presence of grief without judgment, perhaps sensing it as a pressure or emptiness in your chest, a quiet ache. Now, gently shift your focus to the quiet strength of your own heart, cultivating a soft, warm kindness – agape – not just for yourself, but for all who experience the profound ache of loss. Imagine this kindness as a gentle, radiant light emanating from your chest, soothing and comforting. For the next five to ten minutes, breathe in any tension or sorrow you feel, and as you exhale, radiate this tender kindness outwards, allowing it to soften the edges of your own grief and extend to others who are hurting. Conclude by dedicating this moment of open-hearted presence and connection to the well-being of all beings, knowing that even in sorrow, a deep and abiding connection remains.
Transforming Use the energy as fuel — redirect toward wisdom
To work with grief through transformation: Sit with the raw, potent energy of your grief, perhaps feeling its intensity as a constriction in your throat or a pressure behind your eyes. Instead of recoiling, lean into this sensation, recognizing its depth as a testament to profound connection and love – the very wellspring from which it arises. Consider the journey of the Bolivian cacao farmers, who channeled their grief over environmental degradation into a powerful, unified stand for their land. Allow the keen edge of your personal sorrow to sharpen your awareness of suffering in the world, particularly those who grieve similar losses. Breathe into the heart of your grief, and as you do, let its energy expand into a powerful aspiration for the relief of all beings. This isn't about denying your pain, but about recognizing its universal resonance and using its transformative heat to fuel a boundless metanoia, a turning of the heart towards universal compassion and active care.
Transcending Look directly — allow self-liberation
To work with grief through transcendence: Settle into a natural, open awareness, allowing your mind to rest without effort. When grief arises, do not push it away or cling to its narrative. Instead, turn your gaze directly towards the very sensation of grief itself, wherever it manifests in your body or mind—perhaps as a dull ache, a sharp pang, or a pervasive emptiness. Ask yourself: What is its true nature? Does it have a solid core, a fixed location, a color, a shape, a size that can be pinpointed? Can you grasp it as a separate, enduring thing? As you investigate with this gentle, unwavering attention, observe how the perceived solidity of grief begins to dissolve under the direct light of awareness, revealing its inherent insubstantiality, its empty yet luminous quality. Like a wave in the ocean, it arises, endures, and passes, without a separate, enduring self. Rest in this recognition of its boundless, unfabricated nature, allowing the raw energy of grief to self-liberate into the vast, open expanse of your natural mind, leaving no trace behind, only open space.

These 80-Year-Olds Have the Memory of 50-Year-Olds. Scientists Now Know Why.
What has happened
SuperAgers — people over 80 whose memory performance matches adults at least 30 years younger — challenge the assumption that cognitive decline is inevitable. A 25-year study at Northwestern Medicine, examining 77 donated SuperAger brains and tracking 290 participants, identified two distinct protective mechanisms: resistance (brains do not develop harmful plaques and tangles associated with Alzheimer's) and resilience (some develop these proteins but their brains remain functionally unaffected). Structurally, SuperAger cortexes show little to no thinning with age, and their anterior cingulate cortexes — supporting decision-making and emotional regulation — are sometimes thicker than those of younger adults. They also possess higher numbers of von Economo neurons and larger entorhinal neurons critical for memory. Most SuperAgers share strong social connections and sustained mental engagement.
