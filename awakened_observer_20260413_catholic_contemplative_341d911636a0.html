Nigerian Air Force Kills Over 100 Civilians at Weekly Market
What has happened
A Nigerian Air Force jet strike aimed at Boko Haram militants hit the Jilli weekly market in Yobe State on Saturday, killing at least 100 civilians and injuring scores more, including children. The Yobe State Government confirmed the military was targeting a jihadist stronghold near the market when people who had come to trade were caught in the blast. Amnesty International, which verified the toll from survivors and medical sources, has called for an independent investigation. Some local accounts put the death toll as high as 200.
ABC News / Amnesty International ↗
✦

They came to sell bread, not to wage war. They came carrying yams, bolts of cloth, the small transactions that hold a community together. One hundred of them are dead now — caught not in a battle but in a market. Jesus spent most of his ministry in places like this: busy with commerce, alive with the daily weight of earning and feeding and living.

There is a line in Matthew that returns here: 'Whatever you did for the least of these, you did for me.' Not symbolically — literally. The people of the Jilli market were the least of these. Their deaths are not distant news. They are a direct address to anyone who claims to follow the Gospel.

What do we do with this? Not guilt, which collapses into paralysis. But the metanoia the Gospel asks of us: a genuine turning. A decision to see these deaths, to name them, and to let them cost us something — even in small ways — as those who believe every human life is sacred beyond measure.

— In the spirit of the Gospel of Matthew's teaching on the dignity of the least of these

Grief & Loss Practice

Find a quiet moment and open your hands in your lap — the gesture of receiving. Breathe slowly. Bring to mind the people of the Jilli market: the sellers, the buyers, the children at the edge of the stalls. Do not look away. Let their faces — imagined, unknown — be real to you.

Ask yourself one question, slowly: What would it mean to believe that each of these lives is as precious as my own? Sit with that for three minutes. Let it be a prayer that doesn't require a quick answer.

🌅 Kingdom of God View  ·  Christian — Gospel

The kingdom of God is not a future state. Jesus announced it as present, available, breaking in — 'the kingdom of God is at hand.' But at hand to whom, and where?

In Matthew 25, Jesus locates the kingdom precisely: among the hungry, the stranger, the prisoner, the sick. Not in palaces or parliaments. In the market at Jilli, Yobe State, where people came to buy and sell the small necessities of life, the kingdom was present — in the transactions of ordinary human dignity, in the choosing and counting of coins, in the companionship of the weekly gathering.

The bomb fell on the kingdom. Not on an abstraction. On the specific place where Jesus said he would be found.

This is not theodicy — an attempt to explain why God permits such things. It is a statement about where our attention must go. If the kingdom is among the poor and the vulnerable, then the church's vocation is not first to explain suffering but to be present to it, to name it, and to work — with whatever tools are available — toward a world that does not make such choices.

— Kingdom of God view: the kingdom present in the marketplace, among the ordinary and the poor

Russia and Ukraine Accuse Each Other of 2,000+ Easter Ceasefire Violations
What has happened
A 32-hour ceasefire declared by President Putin for Orthodox Easter broke down within hours, with Ukraine's military reporting 2,299 violations — including 747 attack drone strikes and 1,045 FPV drone strikes — by Sunday morning. Russia's Defense Ministry also claimed 1,971 violations by Ukrainian forces. Both countries continued shelling and drone campaigns throughout the Easter period, and civilians were reported killed on the Russian side of the border. A similar truce last Easter also collapsed into mutual accusations.
Al Jazeera / PBS NewsHour ↗
✦

Easter is the feast of impossibility made real. And yet within the first hour of the truce, 747 drone strikes. Russia and Ukraine together logged nearly four thousand ceasefire violations before Sunday Mass had ended. What does the Resurrection mean in a world where even its feast cannot hold a ceasefire?

It means precisely what it always meant: not that suffering ends, but that it does not have the final word. The Church has no army, no drone fleet. But she has a voice, and that voice must say clearly: this is a desecration. The deliberate continuation of violence on the holiest day of the Christian year, whatever uniform one wears, is a sin against humanity and against hope itself.

And yet the deeper question remains for each of us: what ceasefire can I hold within my own life, my own heart? John XXIII wrote that peace is not merely the absence of war. It is an order built by love. That order must begin now — in us — in the only moment we have.

— In the spirit of Catholic social teaching on peace, conscience, and the dignity of every human person

Helplessness & Overwhelm Practice

In the tradition of lectio divina, take this line from the Easter liturgy slowly: 'Do not be afraid. He is not here. He has risen.' Read it once. Then read it again and let it touch the specific fear or grief you are carrying today.

On a third reading, ask: What in me needs to rise? What am I ready to let die so that something truer can live? Sit in silence for five minutes. This is resurrection as practice, not merely as belief.

🫙 Kenosis View  ·  Christian mystical

Kenosis is Paul's word in Philippians 2: Christ 'emptied himself, taking the form of a servant.' It is the theological name for the self-emptying that makes genuine encounter with the other possible.

Every failed ceasefire is a failure of kenosis. Each side clings to its grievances, its strategic advantage, its narrative of victimhood and justified response. The 2,299 violations are not crimes of aggression only — they are acts of self-preservation, of refusing to be the one who gives first.

Kenosis does not mean unilateral surrender. Thomas Merton was careful here: genuine self-emptying is not self-destruction. It is the release of the defended self, the one that cannot afford to be wrong, that cannot afford to give without a guarantee of reciprocation.

What would a kenotic foreign policy look like? We do not know — it has never been tried at scale. But every peace that has ever held has contained its element: the moment when one party chose, against strategic logic, to extend the hand without knowing if it would be taken. Easter is the feast of that gesture. And so the ceasefire was declared. And so it broke. And so Easter comes again next year.

— Kenosis view: self-emptying as the theological ground of peace — the gesture offered without guarantee of return

Hungary's Péter Magyar Defeats Viktor Orbán, Ending 16 Years of Authoritarian Rule
What has happened
Hungary's parliamentary election on April 12 produced a historic result: opposition leader Péter Magyar's Tisza party won 53.6% of the vote, securing 138 of 199 parliamentary seats — enough for a supermajority — while Viktor Orbán's Fidesz took just 55 seats with 37.8%. Turnout reached 77%, the highest since Hungary's post-communist era. Orbán, in power for 16 consecutive years and long seen as a symbol of European democratic backsliding, conceded defeat on election night. Magyar is expected to reverse many of Orbán's constitutional changes and restore Hungary's ties to the European Union.
Al Jazeera / CNN ↗
✦

Merton spent years in a monastery in Kentucky, watching the world from what he called 'the margin of history.' But he was never indifferent to it. When he heard about Budapest in 1956, he wept. He believed contemplative life was not a withdrawal from history but a different way of being fully inside it.

In Hungary, 77 percent of the people came out and chose differently. After sixteen years, something in the national body remembered its own dignity. Merton would recognize this. He called it the 'true self' — not the self constructed by power and fear and years of managed reality, but the self that knows it is free because it is held in something greater than any government.

Elections are not, in themselves, awakening. But something in this one moves in the direction of the true self: the refusal to keep pretending that what is happening is acceptable. That refusal — en masse, on a ballot — is not kenosis. But it rhymes with it. It is the beginning of an unlearning.

— In the spirit of Thomas Merton's teaching on the true self and the contemplative engagement with history

Hope & Renewal Practice

Merton wrote: 'The beginning of contemplation is the beginning of freedom.' Today, recall a moment in your own life when you chose differently — when you refused, quietly, to go along with what you knew to be false. Not a dramatic act. A small one.

Take two minutes and sit with that memory. Honor it. Let it remind you who you are beneath what you perform for the world. That person — the one who refused — is closer to your true self than anything else you have done today.

🌅 Kingdom of God View  ·  Christian — Gospel

The kingdom of God, in Jesus's teaching, is not imposed from above. It grows — like a mustard seed, like leaven in dough — from within the ordinary substance of human life. It is not a government program. But sometimes it moves through governments.

When 77 percent of a people, after sixteen years, choose differently — not out of optimism but out of a deep refusal to continue consenting to what they know is wrong — something in that refusal is consonant with the kingdom. Not identical to it. But consonant.

The language of the Gospel for this is metanoia: the turning of the whole person, the reversal of direction. In Hungary, something like collective metanoia took place. Not religious metanoia — political. But the structure is the same: the recognition that what has been accepted as inevitable is not, in fact, inevitable. That another way is possible. That the door that appeared to be sealed is, actually, open.

'Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness' — not for comfort, not for stability, but for what is right. In Hungary, a great many people were hungry and thirsty. They ate and drank on April 12th.

— Kingdom of God view: collective metanoia as a political form of the Gospel's deepest invitation

Trump Orders U.S. Naval Blockade of Strait of Hormuz After Iran Talks Collapse
What has happened
President Trump ordered the U.S. Navy to begin blockading the Strait of Hormuz effective Monday after U.S.-Iran peace talks in Islamabad collapsed on Sunday. Roughly 20% of the world's oil and natural gas transits the strait, and markets responded with Brent crude prices jumping 10–13% in early trading. U.S. Central Command clarified the blockade targets ships paying tolls to Iran, not all vessels using the waterway. The United Kingdom declined to participate, stating the strait is 'urgently needed to support the global economy,' while Russia and China vetoed a UN Security Council resolution on the crisis.
NPR / CNBC ↗
✦

Francis once walked to Egypt in the middle of the Crusades to speak with Sultan Malik al-Kamil. He brought no weapons, only his willingness to be in the presence of the other. The Sultan received him with courtesy. They talked for days. Nothing was resolved politically, but something human happened between them that armies had not managed in years of fighting.

Now the Strait of Hormuz is closed, and a 10 percent jump in oil prices means that someone, somewhere, will go without — will heat their home less, cut the food budget, face a choice they shouldn't have to make. Creation is not abstract. The poor are not abstract. They feel the tremors of decisions made in rooms they will never enter.

Francis believed that voluntary simplicity — the love of poverty freely chosen — was not self-punishment but freedom. A freedom that makes the blockade of a strait matter less to you, because you have already loosened your grip on what you think you need. This is not a political strategy. But it is the contemplative ground from which better politics can grow.

— In the spirit of Saint Francis of Assisi's teaching on holy poverty, Sister Creation, and peace as a personal vocation

Anxiety & Uncertainty Practice

Francis prayed: 'Lord, make me an instrument of your peace.' Not an observer of peace, or a mourner of its absence — an instrument.

Today, identify one specific thing you actually control — in your household, your spending, your consumption — that you could loosen your grip on. Not out of guilt. Out of freedom. Even a small act of voluntary simplicity, chosen consciously, is a form of prayer. It is also, quietly, a refusal of the logic that closed the strait.

🫙 Kenosis View  ·  Christian mystical

The blockade of the Strait of Hormuz is, at its root, an act of the defended self writ large: the self that cannot tolerate uncertainty, that responds to failed negotiation with the assertion of control, that mistakes power for security.

Kenosis moves in the opposite direction. It releases the claim to control what cannot ultimately be controlled. It discovers — not through naivety but through genuine inner transformation — that security is not a function of domination.

This sounds impractical in geopolitics. And yet: the most durable peace agreements in history have contained this element. Camp David. The Good Friday Agreement. Moments when leaders chose, against every strategic calculation, to let go of something they believed essential to their security — and discovered that the other side, also terrified, also defended, could respond in kind.

Anxiety is the klesha of the defended self. It arises from the belief that if we loosen our grip, we will lose everything. Kenosis says: loosen the grip. Not because the world is safe, but because the defended self never finds the security it seeks. The Strait will still be there. But what you are will have changed.

— Kenosis view: releasing the defended self as the ground of a security that domination can never provide
