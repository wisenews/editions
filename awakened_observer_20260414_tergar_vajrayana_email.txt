  [IMPERMANENCE & PASSING]
  Legendary mountaineer Jim Whittaker, first American to summit
  Everest, dies at 97

  Jim Whittaker, who made history on May 1, 1963, as the first
  American to summit Everest, died on April 7, 2026, at his home
  in Port Townsend, Washington. He was 97. Whittaker led REI for
  decades and remained an icon of American outdoor life. His
  success on Everest depended on Sherpa guide Nawang Gombu, who
  reached the summit alongside him. His death closes a chapter in
  mountaineering history and invites reflection on achievement,
  legacy, and impermanence.

  ✦

  Jim Whittaker stood on Everest on May 1, 1963. He did not stand
  alone. Nawang Gombu stood beside him, and beneath them both
  stood a mountain older than human memory, and beneath the
  mountain stood the slow arithmetic of plates grinding into sky.
  Now, at 97, Whittaker has dissolved into what was always holding
  him. This is the summit beyond the summit — the one no one
  plants a flag on, because there is no one left to plant it. When
  we grieve a life like this, something tender happens. We feel
  the loss, yes. But if we look gently, we also feel how much of
  him was never separate from Gombu, from the rope, from the wind,
  from us reading about him today. Grief, looked at with kindness,
  begins to show us the opposite of what we feared. The person is
  gone. The connections are not. That, too, is a kind of summit.
  — In the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teaching on impermanence as intimacy

  FROM THE TRADITION  [PARAPHRASE]

  "Whatever arises, do not reject it. Whatever fades, do not chase
  it. Rest in the awareness that sees both coming and going, and
  you will find you have never moved."
  — Paraphrase in the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teachings on awareness and impermanence

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  SOCRATES: Tell me, Mingyur — when you say Whittaker has
  'dissolved,' do you mean he has ceased, or that we were
  mistaken about what he was?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: The second, mostly. A wave does not cease
  when it flattens into the sea. It only stops pretending to be
  separate from it. Whittaker was like that. So is Gombu. So are
  you and I, speaking now.
  SOCRATES: Yet the newspapers report a death. A wife weeps. A
  grandson will never hear the old stories again in the old
  voice. Surely something has ended.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Yes. The particular configuration has ended.
  That is real, and grief is the honest response to it. I do not
  ask anyone to skip the tears. I only ask — when the tears come
  — to notice what the tears are made of.
  SOCRATES: And what are they made of?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Love. And love requires two. If the grief is
  still moving in you, then something of him is still moving in
  you. The Greeks knew this — you had a word, did you not, for
  the dead who live on in us?
  SOCRATES: We spoke of the soul, and of memory as its home. But
  I confess I never climbed a mountain. Does the summit teach
  what the agora cannot?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: The summit teaches that the view is
  borrowed. You cannot take it down with you. You carry only
  what you were already carrying — the friend who climbed beside
  you, the breath that was never yours. Whittaker knew this. He
  always said Gombu got there first in every way that mattered.
  SOCRATES: Then perhaps the examined life and the examined
  mountain end at the same place — in the recognition that we
  were never the one climbing.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Yes. And that recognition is where the grief
  becomes gratitude, without stopping being grief.
  — In the spirit of the Tibetan Buddhist and Socratic
  traditions in dialogue

  PRACTICE  [Grief & Loss]

  Sit upright, hands resting. Bring to mind someone you have lost
  — a person, a role, a version of yourself. Feel the ache in the
  chest. Do not push it away. On the in-breath, breathe that ache
  in as warm, dark smoke. On the out-breath, send out cool light
  to everyone who has ever lost what you have lost — which is
  everyone. Ten minutes. This is tonglen transforming grief into
  connection. Close by placing your hand over the heart and
  silently saying: 'What loved me has not left me. It has become
  the space I am sitting in.' This is the transforming method:
  grief as the doorway into the fabric we never stopped being part
  of.

  MAHAMUDRA VIEW VIEW

  From the ground of awareness, no one ever reached the summit,
  because there was never a separate climber and never a separate
  mountain. The one who stood on Everest in 1963 was already the
  space in which Everest appeared. The one who died on April 7 was
  already the space in which death appears. Grief arises in that
  same space, and has the same nature as the snow at 29,000 feet —
  vivid, cold, utterly empty of solidity. Do not console yourself.
  Do not reach for the next thought. Look directly at the grief
  and ask: where is it? The looking itself is rigpa. The grief,
  seen, is already rang grol, self-liberated. Whittaker has not
  gone anywhere. Neither have you.
  — Mahamudra view: grief as the self-display of awareness

  [CULTURE & MEANING]
  Nepal's Foreign Minister invokes Buddhist dependent origination
  and compassion at global diplomacy forum

  Speaking on April 11, 2026, at the Indian Ocean Conference in
  Port Louis, Mauritius, Nepal Foreign Minister Shisir Khanal
  invoked pratityasamutpada — the Buddhist principle of dependent
  origination — as a framework for international relations. He
  called for karuna (compassion) from those wielding power,
  extending ethical concern beyond human politics to marine and
  terrestrial ecosystems. Khanal connected this vision to Lumbini,
  the birthplace of the Buddha. The speech represents a rare
  instance of Buddhist philosophy articulated at the level of
  multilateral foreign policy.

  ✦

  In Mauritius, at a conference of states and their anxieties,
  Nepal's Foreign Minister spoke pratityasamutpada (dependent
  origination) and karuna (compassion) into the microphone as
  though they were not exotic. They are not exotic. They are
  simply accurate. Many of us carry a quiet confusion — we think
  the dharma is for the cushion, and the world is for something
  else, something harder and more realistic. This is a mistake.
  The world is not harder than the dharma; the world is what the
  dharma is describing. When a statesman names the interdependence
  of oceans and nations and creatures in one breath, he is not
  being poetic. He is being exact. The confusion that separates
  practice from policy, retreat from responsibility, dissolves a
  little when we hear this. Lumbini is not only a pilgrimage site.
  It is a reminder that wisdom once entered the world through a
  woman leaning on a tree, and has been trying to get back out
  into the world ever since.
  — In the spirit of the Dalai Lama's teaching on secular ethics rooted in interdependence

  FROM THE TRADITION  [EXACT QUOTE]

  "If you think you are too small to make a difference, try
  sleeping with a mosquito."
  — The Dalai Lama, widely attributed

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  DALAI LAMA: My old friend, a foreign minister has said your
  words in a room full of ambassadors. Pratityasamutpada, karuna
  — at a podium, not on a cushion.
  SHANTIDEVA: Then the Bodhicaryavatara has walked further than
  I dared hope. But tell me — did they understand him, or did
  they applaud politely and return to their portfolios?
  DALAI LAMA: Both, I suspect. But that is how the dharma has
  always moved in the world — half-heard, half-lived, and still
  working. A seed does not need to be understood by the soil to
  grow.
  SHANTIDEVA: True. Yet I confess a worry. The word karuna,
  spoken without practice, can become a cosmetic. It can
  decorate a policy that remains cruel.
  DALAI LAMA: This is the real danger, yes. But consider — for a
  young diplomat in that room, for a junior minister from a
  small country, for a journalist listening, the seed has now
  been planted in language. Later, when the world breaks their
  heart, they may remember the word. And then they may look for
  the practice behind it.
  SHANTIDEVA: That is the bodhisattva's patience. We plant
  without knowing which seeds will sprout.
  DALAI LAMA: And we do not plant only for the sprouts. We plant
  because planting is what a bodhisattva does. The soil is not
  our concern.
  SHANTIDEVA: You are gentler than I was, old friend. I was
  harder on hypocrisy.
  DALAI LAMA: You were younger. And you had not yet been in
  exile for sixty-five years. Exile teaches you that every
  sincere word, anywhere, is a small homeland.
  SHANTIDEVA: Then may every sincere word be the first step. And
  may the diplomats of the world mistake compassion for weakness
  long enough to try it — and discover, too late to turn back,
  that it was strength.
  DALAI LAMA: Tashi delek, old friend. Keep writing.
  — In the spirit of the Gelug and Madhyamaka traditions in
  dialogue

  PRACTICE  [Confusion & Complexity]

  Sit for ten minutes. Bring to mind one decision you face this
  week — small or large, personal or public. Instead of asking
  'what do I want,' ask three times, slowly: 'Who is already
  affected by this? Who will be? Who am I not seeing?' Let the web
  of the question widen with each asking. Then place your right
  hand over your heart and whisper: 'May my choice be the choice
  of everything this choice touches.' This is transforming
  confusion: the energy of not-knowing is redirected into the
  wider seeing that bodhichitta already is. The confusion does not
  vanish. It becomes the felt edge of the larger intelligence.

  BODHICHITTA VIEW VIEW

  From the bodhisattva's view, there is no such thing as 'foreign'
  policy. There is only the one policy — that no being be left
  outside the circle. When a minister names dependent origination
  at a multilateral forum, the vow has walked into a room it has
  been trying to enter for two and a half millennia. Do not be
  cynical about it. Cynicism is the confusion that has given up.
  Instead, widen. The fisheries of the Indian Ocean, the monsoon
  that waters Kerala, the child in Lumbini who will study this
  minister's speech in thirty years — all of them are already
  inside the vow. Your practice today includes them. It always
  did. You just had not yet been told out loud, in the language of
  states.
  — Bodhichitta view: the widening circle of the vow

  [CONFLICT & IMPERMANENCE]  [LESS REPORTED]
  Drone strike kills at least 30 civilians at wedding celebration
  in North Darfur, Sudan

  On April 10, 2026, a drone strike hit a wedding celebration in
  Kutum, North Darfur, Sudan, killing at least 30 civilians; some
  accounts place the toll as high as 56, including women and
  children. Local rights organisations attributed the attack to
  the Sudanese Armed Forces. The UN condemned the strike as
  unacceptable, noting civilians continue to pay the highest cost
  of a conflict entering its fourth year. The attack received
  minimal sustained Western media coverage, as Sudan's war remains
  largely invisible to international audiences.

  ✦

  A wedding in Kutum. Music, probably. A bride. Relatives who had
  travelled far. Then a drone, and thirty people — maybe fifty-six
  — were suddenly nowhere. And most of us in the West will not
  hear this story named aloud tomorrow, or next week. I want to
  say that clearly, because the invisibility is part of the
  suffering. When I sit with a story like this, anger comes.
  Sharp, hot, righteous. This is shenpa — the hook. The mind grabs
  a side, a villain, a headline. If I stay there, the anger just
  burns me and does nothing for Kutum. But if I look directly at
  the anger, without pushing it away, I find something underneath
  it that is not anger. It is heartbreak. Anger is heartbreak in
  armour. Tonglen removes the armour slowly. It does not make the
  drone un-happen. It makes me someone who can stay in the room
  with what happened, instead of turning the channel.
  — In the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teaching on tonglen and the armour of outrage

  FROM THE TRADITION  [PARAPHRASE]

  "When you recognize that the angry thought has no more substance
  than a cloud, you do not need to fight the cloud. You only need
  to stop mistaking it for the sky."
  — Paraphrase in the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teachings on working with emotion

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  SOCRATES: Mingyur, I have been sitting with this story from
  Kutum for an hour. I am angry. Is this a failure of practice?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: No. It would be a failure not to feel it.
  Anger at this is the correct first response of a heart that is
  working. The practice begins with the second response — with
  what you do with the anger once it is here.
  SOCRATES: Then let me practise by questioning. Who are we
  angry at? The pilot? The general? The government? The arms
  manufacturer? The algorithm that chose not to put Kutum in our
  feeds?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Notice — each time you name a target, the
  anger grows a little sharper and a little narrower. Shenpa.
  The hook. It feels like justice but it is actually
  contraction.
  SOCRATES: So should we not seek accountability? Surely the
  Athenian in me cannot let that go.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Accountability, yes. Absolutely. But
  accountability is a long, patient work of institutions and
  courts and journalism. It is not what is happening in your
  chest right now. What is happening in your chest right now is
  shenpa, and if you do not work with it, it will exhaust you
  long before any court is convened.
  SOCRATES: So the practice is — what? To be angry more gently?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: To be angry more accurately. The anger,
  looked at directly, reveals itself to be grief. The grief,
  stayed with, reveals itself to be love — love for strangers
  whose names you will never know. That love is what will keep
  you reading the next story from Sudan, and the next, and the
  next, when the rest of the world has moved on.
  SOCRATES: Then outrage, unexamined, is the enemy of sustained
  attention.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Yes. And tonglen is the examination.
  — In the spirit of the Tibetan Buddhist and Socratic
  traditions in dialogue

  PRACTICE  [Anger & Aversion]

  Sit. Steady the breath. Bring Kutum to mind — a wedding, music
  cut short, ordinary people. Feel the anger rise. Do not suppress
  it and do not feed it. On the in-breath, breathe in the heavy
  smoke of that wedding — the terror, the grief of families, the
  invisibility of their names in our news. On the out-breath, send
  cool light and steadiness to every person in Darfur tonight, and
  to everyone anywhere who is invisible to the world's attention.
  Fifteen minutes. Close by saying their names aloud if you know
  any. If you do not, say: 'I do not know your names. I do not
  look away.' This is transforming: anger becomes the fuel of
  staying present.

  PURE PERCEPTION VIEW VIEW

  Pure perception does not say the drone was sacred. It says the
  thirty people were. Are. Their wedding was a gathering of
  buddhas who did not know they were buddhas, which does not make
  them any less so. The bride putting on her veil that morning was
  a dakini (a feminine principle of awakened energy). The uncle
  laughing over tea was Manjushri (the bodhisattva of wisdom). The
  cousin filming on his phone was already, in that moment,
  transmitting the dharmakaya, though no one would call it that.
  Pure perception does not ask us to feel better about Kutum. It
  asks us to notice that every face our news ignores is a face the
  ground of reality does not ignore. Not one was invisible to
  awareness itself. That recognition, held steadily, is what makes
  us incapable of looking away.
  — Pure perception view: the sacred in the unseen

  [CONFLICT & IMPERMANENCE]
  US-Iran nuclear talks collapse; Trump orders naval blockade of
  Iranian ports

  High-level US-Iran talks in Islamabad on April 11-12, 2026,
  collapsed after more than 21 hours, with both sides reaching
  roughly 80% of a framework before breaking down over nuclear
  terms, Strait of Hormuz access, and frozen assets. On April 13,
  President Trump ordered a naval blockade of Iranian ports,
  escalating military pressure. A ceasefire nominally holds but
  violations continue. The breakdown marks the failure of the most
  significant US-Iran diplomatic engagement in decades, leaving
  tens of millions in the region under threat of further
  escalation.

  ✦

  Twenty-one hours. Eighty percent agreement. And then the
  remaining twenty percent — the Strait, the assets, the word
  'verification' — became too heavy to carry across the finish
  line, and the whole thing fell. Now ships are moving into
  position, and tens of millions of people who were not in that
  room in Islamabad will pay the price of what happened there.
  When I read a story like this, I do not feel anger at any single
  delegation. I feel sorrow. Both sides came to that table because
  they were afraid. Both sides left that table still afraid, and
  now more so. The negotiators are suffering beings. The citizens
  who will bear the blockade are suffering beings. Even the
  leaders who ordered it are suffering beings, though they hide it
  behind very hard faces. Eighty percent is not a failure — it is
  evidence. Evidence that the conditions for agreement exist. What
  collapsed was not the agreement. What collapsed was the
  willingness, for one afternoon, to hold position more lightly
  than life. That willingness can return. It has before.
  — In the spirit of the Dalai Lama's teaching on conflict as shared suffering

  FROM THE TRADITION  [PARAPHRASE]

  "My enemy is my greatest teacher, because only he can give me
  the practice of patience."
  — The Dalai Lama, widely taught in his public teachings on tolerance

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  DALAI LAMA: Shantideva, you wrote that anger destroys in one
  moment the merit of a thousand kalpas. I think of Islamabad.
  Twenty-one hours of work, then one hardening, and the thing
  was gone.
  SHANTIDEVA: The teaching holds. But I wrote it for monks on
  cushions, not for ministers at tables. Does it apply?
  DALAI LAMA: It applies more, not less. A monk's anger costs
  him his practice. A minister's anger costs hundreds of
  thousands their electricity, their medicine, their sons. The
  stakes scale.
  SHANTIDEVA: Then why did your ninth chapter on wisdom not
  persuade them?
  DALAI LAMA: Because the ninth chapter has not yet been put on
  the agenda of any foreign ministry. Perhaps it is my fault. I
  have been polite for too long.
  SHANTIDEVA: I was not polite. I wrote that patience is the one
  armour that cannot be pierced, and I meant it as strategy, not
  only as ethics.
  DALAI LAMA: Yes. Patience is not weakness. Patience is the
  refusal to let the other side's fear become your own fear by
  contagion. In Islamabad, the fear jumped across the table in
  the last hours. Both sides caught it. Both sides then reached
  for what they always reach for.
  SHANTIDEVA: And the blockade?
  DALAI LAMA: The blockade is fear wearing a uniform. It does
  not frighten me less because it is dressed formally. But I
  will not be infected by it. My job is to remain a place where
  the eighty percent can be remembered — until someone is ready
  to return to the table.
  SHANTIDEVA: Then your practice and mine are the same, old
  friend. We are custodians of the unfinished agreement.
  DALAI LAMA: Yes. Someone must hold the door open. Otherwise
  history forgets it was ever open at all.
  — In the spirit of the Gelug and Madhyamaka traditions in
  dialogue

  PRACTICE  [Anger & Aversion]

  Sit for twelve minutes. Bring to mind someone with whom you
  currently disagree — not an abstraction, a real person. Feel
  where in the body the disagreement lives. On the in-breath,
  breathe in the heat of your own position. On the out-breath,
  breathe out the recognition: 'They are afraid of what I am
  afraid of, approached from the other side.' Repeat until the
  heat softens even slightly. Close by bringing to mind the
  negotiators in Islamabad, the sailors now at sea, the families
  in Tehran and the families in American naval towns — all of them
  — and send one warm breath toward all of them equally. This is
  transforming anger: the energy of position becomes the fuel of
  empathy.

  MAHAMUDRA VIEW VIEW

  From the ground of awareness, there are no ships at Hormuz.
  There is only the display — vivid, loud, consequential, and
  empty of any essence that could be found if you looked. This is
  not denial. People will suffer. The suffering, too, is display —
  sharp, real in its appearance, empty in its nature. The rage at
  the failed talks, the relief of the hawks, the grief of the
  mediators — each is a movement of mind, arising and dissolving
  in the same ground. Do not flatten the story by saying 'all is
  illusion.' That is a misunderstanding. Say instead: every
  hardened position in that room was tsal, the dynamic energy of
  awareness, mistaken for something solid. Recognition does not
  end the blockade. It ends our participation in the delusion that
  the blockade is separate from the mind that ordered it.
  — Mahamudra view: hardened position as tsal mistaken for substance

  [INTERDEPENDENCE & ECONOMY]
  US unemployment rises to 4.4% as job postings hit post-pandemic
  low and workers face deepening insecurity

  US unemployment has risen to 4.4% as of April 2026, while job
  openings have fallen to 6.54 million — a post-pandemic low.
  March 2026 produced only 178,000 new jobs, and underemployment
  affects roughly 8% of the labour force. Youth unemployment (ages
  16-24) exceeds 10%. The deterioration spans sectors, with
  workers describing the labour market as having shifted from
  opportunity to survival. The economic anxiety is widespread but
  its spiritual and existential dimensions receive little
  sustained attention.

  ✦

  4.4 percent. 6.54 million openings. 178,000 jobs. These are not
  just numbers. They are the shape of a specific feeling, carried
  in millions of bodies right now — the tightening in the chest
  when you open the laptop, the dry mouth before a performance
  review, the 3 a.m. arithmetic of the rent. I want to be careful
  here. The facts of insecurity are real. A person who cannot pay
  for groceries is not suffering from 'mind only.' But the
  anxiety-mind that spins around the fact is a second arrow, and
  often it is the one that does the deeper damage. Shenpa is the
  body grabbing hard around not-enough, and once it grabs, it
  cannot think clearly, cannot apply for the next job, cannot be a
  parent who is present at dinner. The facts need addressing
  through action. The anxiety needs addressing through awareness.
  These are two different jobs, and confusing them is part of why
  suffering multiplies.
  — In the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teaching on the two arrows of anxiety

  FROM THE TRADITION  [PARAPHRASE]

  "Anxiety is not the problem. The problem is believing the story
  the anxiety is telling you about the future. The story is never
  as intelligent as the awareness watching it."
  — Paraphrase in the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teachings on panic and awareness

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  SOCRATES: Mingyur, my neighbour has been unemployed four
  months. He asks me what the examined life offers a man who
  cannot pay his rent. I did not know what to say.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: The honest answer is: not rent. The examined
  life does not pay rent. Do not pretend otherwise. His landlord
  is not interested in his insight.
  SOCRATES: Then is practice useless to him?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: No — but it is useful for a different thing
  than he thinks. Practice will not produce a job. Practice will
  produce the steadier mind that is more likely to notice the
  job, to show up well at the interview, to endure the fourth
  'no' without collapsing before the fifth, which may be 'yes.'
  SOCRATES: So practice is instrumental?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: No, that is still not right. Practice is not
  a tool for getting a job. It is a way of being that happens to
  be more survivable in a difficult labour market. The
  difference matters.
  SOCRATES: Let me press you. You speak of security as a story
  the anxiety tells. But was security ever real, even when my
  neighbour was employed?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: This is the sharp question. No. When he had
  the job, he also did not have security — he had the felt sense
  of security, because the paycheque was arriving. The paycheque
  was always one board meeting away from ending. He simply did
  not feel it, until he did.
  SOCRATES: So the loss revealed something that was always true.
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Yes. Which does not make it easier. But it
  does change the nature of the work. The work is not to restore
  an illusion. The work is to find a steadier ground than the
  illusion ever offered.
  SOCRATES: And where is that ground?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: In the awareness that is watching the
  anxiety right now. Not in the future. Never in the future.
  — In the spirit of the Tibetan Buddhist and Socratic
  traditions in dialogue

  PRACTICE  [Anxiety & Uncertainty]

  Sit or stand, feet firmly on the floor. Place one hand on the
  belly, one on the chest. Notice exactly where the financial
  anxiety lives — the chest, the throat, the stomach — and breathe
  into that place three times, slowly. Do not try to fix it. Then
  silently say: 'This is the body doing its job. It is afraid
  because it loves being alive.' Thank it. Now, with the body
  steadier, take one concrete, ten-minute action toward your
  livelihood — send one email, update one line of the resume, call
  one person. Action from steadiness, not from panic. This is
  transforming anxiety: the energy that was tightening becomes the
  energy that moves.

  BODHICHITTA VIEW VIEW

  The bodhisattva does not have the luxury of treating
  unemployment as someone else's problem. The ten percent of young
  people who cannot find work are inside the vow. The single
  mother updating her resume at midnight is inside the vow. The
  laid-off engineer ashamed to tell his parents is inside the vow.
  Your practice today, if you have a job, includes them. Your
  practice today, if you do not, includes every other person who
  does not. Bodhichitta does not require that you solve the labour
  market. It requires that you refuse to let the anxiety isolate
  you inside your own household. Widen. Someone you know is afraid
  about work right now and has not told you. Ask them. That small
  act is the vow walking into the economy.
  — Bodhichitta view: the vow walks into the labour market
