The klesha

Grief manifests as a profound ache, often centered in the chest or throat, accompanied by a sense of hollowness or a pervasive dullness of mind. It’s the raw, visceral experience of absence and longing for what was, or what might have been, as vividly felt by the Bolivian cacao farmers whose ancestral lands were threatened, or the quiet ache for connection in our 'Dry January for your Smartphone' story. This klesha binds us to what is no longer, or what we fear to lose, creating a palpable sense of lack.

The wisdom it reveals

Discriminating Wisdom (pratyavekṣaṇā-jñāna) offers the clarity to discern the precise nature of what has been lost, and what remains, without entanglement. It illuminates the distinct qualities of experience, allowing us to see the boundaries of our sorrow, and the impermanence of both the object of our grief and the grief itself, without denying its reality. This yeshe brings a profound recognition of each phenomenon's unique character, revealing its empty yet vivid manifestation.

How to work with it
Abandoning Apply the antidote — reduce and release
To work with grief through abandoning:

When the wave of grief arises, find its primary sensation in the body – perhaps a tightness in the throat, a pressure behind the eyes, or a dull ache in the chest. Instead of resisting or analyzing the narrative around it, consciously soften around that physical manifestation. Allow the sensation to be present without judgment, letting the edges of the experience relax and expand. Breathe gently into that space, imagining each breath creating more room and ease within the felt experience of grief. Sustain this open, non-resistant presence for five to ten minutes, simply letting the sensation be. As you conclude, gently place a hand over your heart and dedicate this moment of softening and presence to all beings who experience loss, wishing them similar release and comfort. This practice, by reducing resistance, allows the klesha's intensity to naturally diminish.

Transforming Use the energy as fuel — redirect toward wisdom
To work with grief through transformation:

Recognize the intensity of grief not merely as a burden, but as a potent energy that can be redirected. When the ache of loss is strong, sit with it, allowing its full force to be felt. Then, engage in tonglen: as you breathe in, wholeheartedly take in the sorrow, the emptiness, the raw ache of loss – not just your own, but that of all beings who suffer similarly, like the communities impacted by the ongoing conflict in Hormuz. Hold this suffering in the vastness of your open heart, not as a personal burden, but as a universal experience. As you breathe out, send forth a vast space of comfort, peace, and freedom from suffering, wishing all beings relief from their grief. Let the very energy of your personal grief become a boundless wellspring of bodhichitta, extending solace to the entire world, transforming isolation into profound, compassionate connection.

Transcending Look directly — allow self-liberation
To work with grief through transcendence:

When grief grips you, rather than engaging with its narrative or trying to push it away, turn your attention directly to the *experience* of grief itself. Without conceptual overlay, investigate: Where is this 'grief' located in your body or mind? What are its qualities – its color, shape, texture, movement? Is it solid or fluid? Does it have a beginning, middle, or end? Look deeply, like examining a mirage, without seeking to find a concrete entity. As you look, you’ll find no solid, independently existing 'grief' to grasp; it’s a dynamic play of mind, a tsal, arising and dissolving within the vastness of rigpa. Recognize that the apparent solidity of grief is a fabrication of shes pa, not an inherent quality. Rest in this open, luminous awareness, allowing the grief to simply self-liberate (rang grol) back into the dharmakaya, revealing its empty yet vividly manifest nature.

Trump DOJ Indicts Southern Poverty Law Center on 11-Count Federal Fraud Case
What has happened
The Trump Justice Department indicted the Southern Poverty Law Center on 11 federal counts including wire fraud, false statements to banks, and conspiracy to commit money laundering. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche and FBI Director Kash Patel announced the charges on April 21, alleging the SPLC defrauded donors by secretly paying approximately $3 million to informants embedded inside extremist groups between 2014 and 2023, using fictitious corporate bank accounts. The SPLC called the charges political targeting. Legal experts noted that paying confidential informants inside violent groups is standard law enforcement practice, and the case was previously closed under the Biden administration before being revived under Trump. The indictment is widely seen as part of a pattern of the administration using the DOJ against civil society organizations.
Wisdom teaching
✦
Here is an indictment. Here is a prosecutor, an organization, a room of reporters. Beneath this, monks, observe something else: the mind that reads the headline and begins to run. It runs toward what might happen next — to the institutions, to the law, to oneself. This running is anxiety. It is not the indictment. It is the mind leaning into a future that has not arrived, constructing a shape for it, and then suffering that shape as though it were already here. There is suffering in this running. There is an arising: the untrained mind cannot bear uncertainty, and so it manufactures certainty by rehearsing disaster. There is a cessation: uncertainty, met without the running, becomes simply the present moment, in which nothing has yet been decided. And there is a path: the one where attention returns, again and again, to the body, to the breath, to what is actually occurring now.
— In the spirit of the Buddha's teaching on the four noble truths applied to worry

"When the world is filled with evil, transform all mishaps into the path of bodhi."

Sit upright. Place both feet on the floor. Read one news sentence that provokes the anxious running. Then close your eyes and locate, in the body, where the anxiety sits — jaw, sternum, belly. Iran War Day 55: Both Sides Seize Ships as Hormuz Standoff Deepens and Peace Talks Stall
What has happened
On the 55th day since the Iran war began, the conflict is escalating on multiple fronts. Iran seized two foreign commercial vessels and fired on a third in the Strait of Hormuz, while the US Central Command has turned back 31 ships as part of a naval blockade involving approximately 10,000 troops, 17 warships, and over 100 aircraft. Iran parliament refused to reopen the waterway while the US blockade remains, calling it a ceasefire violation. Brent crude oil surpassed $100 per barrel. Peace talks remain stalled: Iran cites the blockade as a breach of commitments; the White House says Trump has set no deadline for a peace proposal. In Lebanon, Israeli airstrikes killed five people including journalist Amal Khalil, and a Red Cross ambulance came under fire.
Wisdom teaching
✦

Warships. Seized vessels. A journalist killed. An ambulance under fire.

Fifty-five days.

Now, for a moment, do not read about the war. Read the mind reading about the war.

There is aversion here. Find it. Where is it? In the jaw? Behind the eyes? A tightening at the center of the chest?

Look at it directly. Not at what it is about. At what it is.

Aversion has no shape. When you look for it, it is not where you thought it was. When you look at where it is not, it is not there either. It was vivid a moment ago. Where did it go?

This is not denial of the war. The war is real. Five people died in Lebanon today. Do not look away.

But the hand that would act on behalf of those five — is that hand clearer when moved by aversion, or when moved by awareness that has looked at aversion and not found it anywhere?

The ground is already free. The war happens on the ground. From the ground, the right action arises naturally, uncomplicated by the one who acts.

Look.

— In the spirit of Tilopa's teaching on direct recognition of the klesha

How Bolivia's Cacao Farmers Took On the Gold-Mining Industry — and Won
What has happened
In Bolivia's Amazon biodiverse heartland, communities of cacao farmers successfully organized against the gold-mining industry threatening their forests and way of life. With gold prices rising globally and fueling environmental destruction across the region, these farming communities — including cacao cultivator Herminio Mamani — organized to pass local laws prohibiting mining in their territory. The victory represents a grassroots achievement: small-scale agricultural communities choosing ecological integrity and long-term land stewardship over the short-term economic pressure of a global commodity boom. The area is home to mahogany forests and rich biodiversity that would have been destroyed by mining operations.
Wisdom teaching
✦

I lived in caves. The mountain was my mother after my first mother died. I know what it is to stand between a forest and those who would take it apart, knowing you may be the one who loses.

Herminio Mamani grows cacao in a forest of mahogany. The gold price rises in cities he has never seen, and men with papers and machines come toward his river. His grief is not the grief that comes after loss. It is the grief that comes before — the grief that knows what is about to be taken, and loves it anyway.

And then — this is the teaching — he did not only grieve. He organized. The grief was the fuel. He let his love for the forest pass through his sorrow and emerge as law, as signature, as neighbors gathered in a small building writing rules.

Grief that stays still becomes bitterness. Grief that moves becomes protection. The same water, the same fire. The cacao tree grows under the mahogany. The small protects the small. The long patience of the forest becomes the long patience of the community.

This is how a mountain teaches.

— In the spirit of Milarepa's teaching on grief as fuel for bodhichitta

Wonder & DiscoveryLess Reported
These 80-Year-Olds Have the Memory of 50-Year-Olds. Scientists Now Know Why.
What has happened
SuperAgers — people over 80 whose memory performance matches adults at least 30 years younger — challenge the assumption that cognitive decline is inevitable. A 25-year study at Northwestern Medicine, examining 77 donated SuperAger brains and tracking 290 participants, identified two distinct protective mechanisms: resistance (brains do not develop harmful plaques and tangles associated with Alzheimer's) and resilience (some develop these proteins but their brains remain functionally unaffected). Structurally, SuperAger cortexes show little to no thinning with age, and their anterior cingulate cortexes — supporting decision-making and emotional regulation — are sometimes thicker than those of younger adults. They also possess higher numbers of von Economo neurons and larger entorhinal neurons critical for memory. Most SuperAgers share strong social connections and sustained mental engagement.
Wisdom teaching
✦

We assume that cognitive decline is inevitable. The SuperAger study quietly undoes the assumption. Eighty-year-olds with the memory of fifty-year-olds — not by genetic accident alone, but, the researchers note, by strong social connections and sustained mental engagement. The brain, it turns out, does not simply run down. It responds to how it is used.

This is a small, specific instance of a larger disease of mind. We believe many things about ourselves and the world without examining them: that aging is decay, that attention cannot be trained, that the mind of eighty is a diminished version of the mind of fifty. These beliefs are not neutral; they shape what we attempt. A person who believes decline is inevitable does not reach for the friend, does not open the book, does not sit in the meditation hall at seventy.

Ignorance, in our tradition, is not stupidity. It is the failure to examine. The antidote is not study for its own sake, but the habit of asking: is this assumption actually so? The SuperAgers offer one small answer. There are many assumptions still to examine.

Socrates: you know least what you assume most. The assumption of decline is so universal it no longer feels like an assumption — it feels like the weather. What Socrates names here, the dharma calls avidya — the ignorance that is not the absence of facts but the absence of examination. Mindfulness is not primarily concentration; it is the light turned on the assumption. Dry January, but for Your Smartphone: What a Month Offline Reveals
What has happened
A journalist and group of participants spent a month deliberately stepping back from smartphone use, documenting what the experience reveals about habitual device dependency. The experiment surfaces the texture of the craving — what it feels like when the automatic reach for the phone is interrupted — and what participants noticed about attention, boredom, presence, and social connection in the absence of the device. The piece frames digital detox not as an ascetic rejection of technology but as a temporary removal that illuminates the ordinarily invisible pull of habitual screen use and the quality of mind available beneath it.
Wisdom teaching
✦

Watch the hand. It goes to the pocket before you ask it to. The phone is not yet out; the reaching has already begun. This reaching is the teacher.

People in the article put the phone down for a month. They reported boredom, restlessness, a strange quiet. Good. That quiet is what the craving had been covering. Not a problem to be solved — a room to be entered.

In the forest, we watched the mind the way you watch weather. A cloud comes. It is not yours. You do not chase it away. You also do not climb inside it. You let it pass, and you note its color, its direction, its weight. The pull toward the screen is a cloud. It comes ten, twenty, a hundred times a day. Each time it comes, you see it a little more clearly. Each time you see it clearly, its hold is a little less.

Craving is not the enemy. Craving is the bell that tells you attention has wandered. Ring the bell, notice, and return. You do not need a month offline. You need one moment, repeated.

— In the spirit of the Forest tradition's teaching on craving as teacher

Practice prescription
Craving & Clinging Practice Transforming
Lojong
"Change your attitude, but remain natural."
For one day, leave the phone in another room between tasks. Each time the hand reaches automatically — toward pocket, toward table, toward the habitual gesture — stop. Do not scold. Simply notice: 'reaching.' Place the hand, instead, on the heart or the belly for three breaths. Notice what the hand was seeking beneath the screen: distraction, company, relief from a small discomfort, a pocket of boredom. The energy of the craving becomes, in those three breaths, the energy of presence. End the day by sitting for five minutes in plain silence, without the device. That silence is the teaching.
Wisdom aspect: Discriminating Wisdom
❤ Appreciative Joy
We rejoice in the mindful courage and clear-eyed self-discipline of those who intentionally step back from habitual engagement, choosing to cultivate a deeper presence and discover the richness of an unmediated experience.
⚗️ Socratic Inversion View  ·  Greek philosophy — Socratic wisdom, received through the dharma

Beware the barrenness of a busy life.

Socrates did not mean the life of toil — he meant the life that mistakes motion for meaning, stimulation for presence. The hand that reaches for the phone thirty, sixty, a hundred times a day is not bored. It is busy. And the experiment reported here — one month of deliberate stillness — confirms what he suspected: beneath the busyness is not emptiness, but a quality of attention that was always available and simply never met.

What Socrates names here, the dharma calls craving's hidden face: the energy that fills every gap before the gap can teach anything. The month offline does not add something. It removes the barrenness that busyness calls richness.

Rest the hand. See what was already there.

— Socratic Inversion view
🫙 Kenosis View  ·  Christian mystical

Kenosis: the self-emptying of Christ, who did not cling to equality with God but emptied himself (Philippians 2). The smartphone in the pocket is a small fullness — a constant availability of stimulation, confirmation, company. To put it down, even for a month, is a minor kenosis. You release the fullness that had been carrying you, and something quieter appears — not an emptiness of lack, but a spaciousness of presence. The clinging exposed is not to the device; it is to the self that needs constant accompaniment. What would kenosis look like in relation to this story today? One pocket emptied, once, between tasks — and the attention it would take to notice what arose in that small vacancy. There is nothing to fill. That is the teaching.

— Kenosis view
