  [THE HUMAN INSIDE THE HEADLINE]  [LESS REPORTED]
  I was bathed with acid meant for my brother – Anambra graduate

  Obanye Francess, a Biochemistry graduate from Anambra, Nigeria,
  became an acid attack survivor at age nine when she was
  mistakenly targeted instead of her brother. The attack, stemming
  from a family conflict, left her with severe burns. Despite
  immense physical pain, emotional trauma, and societal
  discrimination, Francess completed her education. She now
  operates an online dropshipping business, diversifying her
  income to overcome employment challenges faced by burn
  survivors. She also recently experienced the loss of both her
  parents.

  ✦

  The story of Obanye Francess, who endured an acid attack meant
  for another, is heartbreaking. Her journey through immense
  physical pain, emotional trauma, and societal discrimination,
  yet still completing her education, reveals an indomitable
  spirit. It's easy to feel anger or despair when faced with such
  senseless violence, such ma rigpa (ignorance). But within this
  very suffering, if we look closely, we find the raw, untamed
  energy of compassion. This isn't a soft, gentle feeling that
  avoids pain. It's a fierce, courageous willingness to face the
  reality of suffering, both hers and our own, and to connect with
  the profound shared vulnerability of being human. Francess's
  resilience itself is a teaching, a testament to the mind's
  capacity to find strength amidst the most horrific
  circumstances. How do we meet such suffering without being
  overwhelmed?
  — In the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teaching on transforming suffering into wisdom

  FROM THE TRADITION  [PARAPHRASE]

  "The very source of our suffering is also the source of our
  wisdom."
  Mingyur Rinpoche, Joyful Wisdom: Embracing Change and Finding Freedom

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  SOCRATES: Mingyur, this tale of an innocent young woman
  suffering such a cruel fate, inflicted by the very fabric of
  family, stirs a deep disquiet. Where is justice in such a
  world, and how can one find solace amidst such profound and
  senseless pain?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: Ah, Socrates, your questions touch the very
  heart of human experience. Indeed, the pain is real, the
  injustice stark. But if we look only at the surface, we miss
  something crucial. This young woman, Francess, in her enduring
  spirit, despite the horrific circumstances, reveals not just
  suffering, but an incredible, inherent resilience. The
  suffering is not the whole story. It's like a muddy pond; the
  mud is there, yes, but beneath it, the water remains clear.
  The challenge for us is not to deny the mud, but to recognize
  the clear water that is always present. Her journey shows us
  that even in the darkest corners of ma rigpa, the light of our
  true nature, our buddha-nature, is never extinguished. Her
  ability to continue, to learn, to grow, is a vivid display of
  this inherent potential.
  SOCRATES: So, you suggest that within the very crucible of
  suffering, a deeper truth or capacity for strength is
  revealed? It seems counter-intuitive to seek wisdom in such a
  place. One might expect only despair. Yet, your words hint at
  something more profound than mere stoicism. Is this resilience
  a choice, or an innate quality?
  MINGYUR RINPOCHE: It is both, in a way. The choice is in how
  we relate to the experience. When pain arises, the shes pa
  (conceptual mind) often creates a second layer of suffering
  through our reactions – fear, anger, self-pity. But if we can
  rest, even for a moment, with the raw sensation, without
  judgment, we begin to touch something deeper. We touch rigpa,
  the natural state of awareness, which is always present,
  always clear, always open, even amidst the most intense
  kleshas. Francess's journey, her determination to pursue
  education, despite the immense challenges, isn't just a
  choice; it's an expression of this innate, luminous capacity
  of the mind. It’s the tsal (display, energy) of rigpa
  manifesting as courage. By engaging with this story, we can
  learn to transform our own shenpa (attachment to concepts and
  emotions) around suffering, recognizing the inherent wisdom
  within it.
  — In the spirit of Hellenic philosophy and Vajrayana Buddhism

  PRACTICE  [Compassion & Care]
  Lojong: "When the world is filled with evil, transform all mishaps into the path of bodhi."
  Alt: "When the world is filled with evil, transform all mishaps into the path of awakening."

  Find a comfortable seated posture, spine erect yet relaxed.
  Gently bring your attention to your breath, feeling its natural
  rhythm. Now, bring to mind Francess's journey, the suffering she
  endured, and her incredible strength. As you breathe in, imagine
  taking in all the pain, the injustice, the kleshas (afflictive
  emotions) associated with this story, transforming them into
  pure, boundless compassion in your heart. As you breathe out,
  send out warmth, healing, and unwavering courage to Francess and
  all those who suffer from violence. Continue for 5-7 minutes.
  Conclude by resting in the open awareness that holds both
  suffering and strength.

  REJOICING

  We rejoice in Obanye Francess's astonishing resilience and
  indomitable spirit, which shine brightly through unimaginable
  suffering and injustice. Her courage to continue her journey
  inspires us all.

  MAHAMUDRA VIEW VIEW

  From the mahamudra perspective, this event, horrific as it is,
  is understood as a vivid display of tsal, the dynamic energy of
  awareness. The suffering, the injustice, the resilience – these
  are not separate from the ground of being. They are the
  inherent, unceasing play of phenomena, arising, abiding, and
  dissolving within the vast, open space of rigpa. There is no
  external perpetrator or victim truly separate from this luminous
  dance. The mind's habitual patterns of ma rigpa create the
  conditions for such acts, yet even within that deluded display,
  the unconditioned nature of mind remains untouched, pristine.
  This realization doesn't diminish the pain, but rather places it
  within a boundless context, revealing its empty yet luminous
  essence.
  — Seeing events as the dynamic, self-liberated display of awareness

  [WONDER & DISCOVERY]
  Experimental drug doubles one-year survival in pancreatic cancer

  A new experimental drug has demonstrated the ability to double
  the one-year survival rate for individuals with advanced
  pancreatic cancer in a recent trial. This drug functions by
  enhancing the susceptibility of tumors to both chemotherapy and
  the body's immune system. This development is considered a
  notable advancement in the treatment of pancreatic cancer, which
  is known for its aggressive nature and poor prognosis.

  ✦

  This news of an experimental drug doubling the one-year survival
  rate for pancreatic cancer patients is truly wonderful. It shows
  the incredible potential of human intelligence and dedication
  when directed towards alleviating suffering. Science, at its
  best, is a profound expression of compassion, a relentless
  pursuit to understand the causes of suffering and to find
  skillful means to mitigate them. While we recognize that all
  compounded things are impermanent and that suffering is an
  undeniable part of samsara, these advancements offer precious
  moments of relief, extending life and providing opportunities
  for patients and their families. This allows more time for
  practice, for connection, for living with meaning. It inspires a
  deep sense of gratitude for the collective effort that brings
  such hope.
  — In the spirit of the Dalai Lama's teaching on compassion and interdependence

  FROM THE TRADITION  [EXACT QUOTE]

  "My religion is very simple. My religion is kindness."
  The Dalai Lama

  CROSS-TRADITION DIALOGUE
  SHANTIDEVA: Esteemed teacher, this news of a medical
  breakthrough for a dire illness fills one with a profound
  sense of relief and optimism. Is it not a testament to the
  potential for human effort, rooted in compassion, to alleviate
  suffering in this world, even if only temporarily?
  DALAI LAMA: Indeed, Shantideva. This is a truly encouraging
  development, demonstrating the remarkable capacity of human
  intelligence, when coupled with altruistic motivation, to
  bring about positive change. Such scientific endeavors, driven
  by the wish to reduce the pain and prolong the lives of
  others, are a beautiful expression of bodhichitta. While we
  understand the ultimate nature of existence, that all
  phenomena are impermanent and suffering is inherent in
  samsara, this does not mean we should remain passive. On the
  contrary, it compels us to act, to apply all our faculties –
  intellectual, emotional, and spiritual – to diminish suffering
  wherever and whenever we can. This extension of life, even for
  a year, can be profoundly meaningful, offering precious time
  for spiritual practice, for reconciliation, for experiencing
  joy.
  SHANTIDEVA: Yet, even with such advancements, the impermanent
  nature of life and the inevitability of death remain. How do
  we balance this profound hope for relief with the wisdom of
  sunyata (emptiness) and the understanding that no worldly
  solution is ultimate? Does not such hope, however noble, risk
  fostering attachment to temporary existence?
  DALAI LAMA: This is a wise observation, Shantideva. It is
  essential to hold both perspectives. We embrace the skillful
  means (upaya) of medical science with immense gratitude,
  recognizing its immediate benefit. At the same time, we keep
  in mind the deeper truth of impermanence and sunyata. The two
  are not contradictory; they are complementary. Compassion
  motivates us to alleviate immediate suffering, while wisdom
  reminds us of the ultimate reality. The hope generated by such
  discoveries is not a blind attachment to temporary existence,
  but rather an affirmation of the preciousness of human life
  and an opportunity to use that life for greater good. It
  allows individuals to continue their journey, perhaps to
  deepen their understanding of the Dharma, and to connect with
  others. This inspires us to cultivate both wisdom and
  compassion in equal measure.
  — In the spirit of Mahayana Buddhism and compassionate action

  PRACTICE  [Wonder & Awe]

  REJOICING

  BODHICHITTA VIEW VIEW

  From the bodhichitta perspective, news like this ignites a
  fierce joy. Every moment of extended life, every reduction of
  suffering, is a victory for all sentient beings. This scientific
  discovery is not merely a technical achievement; it is a
  manifestation of collective bodhichitta, the aspiration to
  alleviate suffering. The suffering of one is the suffering of
  all, and the relief of one is the relief of all. Our practice,
  then, is to rejoice wholeheartedly in such breakthroughs and to
  dedicate all the merit generated by the efforts of researchers,
  doctors, and patients towards the ultimate liberation of all
  from disease and the causes of suffering.
  — Recognizing the compassionate interdependence of all efforts to heal

  [NATURE & RENEWAL]
  It's Official: Antarctica's Iconic Emperor Penguins Are
  Endangered Due to Climate Change.

  Emperor penguins and Antarctic fur seals have recently been
  reclassified as 'Endangered' species. This official downgrade is
  a direct consequence of the escalating effects of climate
  change, which are significantly impacting their habitats and
  survival rates. The new status underscores the urgent need for
  conservation efforts to protect these iconic inhabitants of the
  polar regions from further decline.

  ✦

  To hear that Emperor penguins, these magnificent, resilient
  beings, are now endangered due to climate change, naturally
  evokes a deep sense of sadness and concern. It highlights the
  profound interconnectedness of all life and the far-reaching
  impact of human actions. It's easy to get caught in shenpa
  around the despair, the overwhelming scale of the problem. Yet,
  even in this somber news, there's an opportunity to recognize
  something deeper. The sheer existence of these creatures, their
  intricate adaptations, their vibrant life force, is a miracle.
  Their struggle serves as a powerful mirror, reflecting the
  fragility and preciousness of life on this planet. It calls us
  to awaken to the clarity of pure perception, to see not just the
  threat, but the inherent, luminous perfection of these beings,
  just as they are.
  — In the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teaching on the luminous nature of phenomena

  [PEACE & POSSIBILITY]
  Israel-Lebanon ceasefire begins as Iran keeps Strait of Hormuz
  gridlocked

  A 10-day ceasefire has been initiated between Israel and
  Hezbollah in Lebanon, providing a temporary cessation of
  hostilities. This truce aims to create a window for negotiations
  to resolve the broader conflict. Simultaneously, the Strait of
  Hormuz is experiencing significant disruption to shipping, as
  Iran reversed its decision to reopen the waterway and engaged
  vessels attempting passage, citing the continued US blockade of
  Iranian ports. These concurrent developments illustrate both
  fragile peace efforts and persistent geopolitical challenges in
  the region.

  ✦

  The news of a temporary ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah,
  however fragile, offers a glimmer of hope amidst profound
  suffering. In situations of long-standing conflict, where anger,
  fear, and historical grievances create layers of ma rigpa, even
  a brief cessation of hostilities is incredibly precious. It’s a
  moment when the habitual patterns of conflict are momentarily
  interrupted, creating a space for reflection, negotiation, and
  perhaps, a deeper understanding of shared humanity. Our practice
  is not to cling to this hope as a permanent solution, which
  would lead to shenpa, but to recognize it as a momentary
  opening, a pause in the storm. It allows us to train our minds
  to cultivate bodhichitta, to wish for true, lasting peace,
  knowing that even the smallest steps toward compassion can
  transform the landscape of suffering.
  — In the spirit of Mingyur Rinpoche's teaching on finding space in conflict
